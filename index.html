Chapter I

Origin — The Woman Who Left the Dark

His mother’s name was Verath. She was drow — full-blooded, surface-side, and entirely deliberate about both. She did not stumble out of the Underdark. She walked out. The distinction mattered to her and she made sure he understood it early: she was not a refugee. She was not lost. She had made a calculation and acted on it, and she did not apologize for the consequences.

She was not warm in the way people expect mothers to be warm. She was precise. She taught him to read a room before he entered it, to know the weight of silence, to understand that charm was a tool and tools were only as good as the hand holding them. He did not always like her. He respected her completely. The difference between those two things is something he learned from her as well.

His father was a human man whose name Zevrin knows and does not use. He was not a bad man. He was simply not built for the life Verath carried with her, and he left when Zevrin was young enough that the leaving registered as weather rather than wound — something that happened to the environment, not to him specifically. He has not looked for him. He has not needed to.

He was raised in the in-between places — not the Underdark, not any settled human city that would have had opinions about what he looked like. Port towns. Trade routes. Margins. Verath moved, and so he moved, and by the time he was old enough to understand that other children had roots, he had already decided roots were for people who didn’t know better.

She gave him the name Zevrin. It is the only thing from her he has kept without question.

Chapter II

The Cartographer’s Lesson — What He Found When He Looked

Verath disappeared when Zevrin was seventeen. Not gradually. Not with warning. One morning she was there and by evening she was not, and nothing she left behind explained the gap.

He looked for her. This is the only time in his adult life he has done something that he would now classify as going in without a map. He was young enough to believe that thoroughness could substitute for information, that if he just covered enough ground something would resolve. It did not work like that.

He found enough to stop looking. He will not say what he found. He has told people, when pressed, that she is gone — not that she is dead, because he does not know that, but gone in the sense of beyond reach, beyond retrieval, beyond the kind of help he is capable of providing. The distinction is precise and he maintains it carefully.

After this he did not fall apart. He made a decision, which is something different. He decided that he would never again move toward something without knowing the terrain first. He has not broken this rule once since.

This is the architecture under everything: the obsessive preparation, the intelligence gathering, the refusal to be surprised. It is not personality. It is policy, adopted at seventeen in response to a specific lesson, and he has never seen a reason to revise it.

Chapter III

The Pact — What He Reached For and What Reached Back

He was nineteen. He was alone, which was not unusual, and he was in a place that did not have a name on any map he had seen, which was also not unusual — he had been moving through the margins since Verath disappeared, doing work that paid and didn’t ask questions and kept him sharp.

Something spoke to him. He does not describe it as a voice. He describes it, when he describes it at all, as a direction — a pull in the architecture of his awareness, like a door he had not noticed becoming visible. He had enough knowledge by then to understand what this was and what it meant and what it would cost.

He negotiated. This is the part he is most certain about and most willing to say plainly: he did not accept what was offered, he responded with terms. He cannot fully articulate what leverage he had in that conversation, but he has always operated from the position that he had some — and the entity accepted terms that a supplicant would not have been offered. He considers this significant.

The pact functions. His magic is real and it is his and he uses it without apology. What he performs in maintaining the connection — the forms, the invocations, the specific textures of how he reaches for power — he does with precision and without devotion, the way a person might maintain a piece of equipment they do not love but cannot afford to lose.

Chapter IV

The Name — Just Zevrin

He goes by one name. When people ask for more he gives them a look that communicates, without hostility, that the question was a mistake. He is not ashamed of either bloodline. He does not claim either fully. Taking a surname would mean choosing a lineage and the lineages available to him both require explanations he has decided are nobody’s business.

Verath gave him the name. That’s enough. That’s the whole inheritance and he has decided it is sufficient.

Chapter V

Where He Is When BG3 Begins

He is in his mid-to-late twenties. He has been moving through the Sword Coast for several years doing work that uses the skills he has — information gathering, negotiation, occasionally the kind of persuasion that requires a patron-backed eldritch blast to close the argument. He is not affiliated with any organization that would claim him publicly. He has arrangements with several that prefer it that way.

He was on his way somewhere specific when the nautiloid took him. He had a plan. He had prepared. He was mid-map, as it were, which is the specific indignity of the illithid abduction, and the one he is least gracious about.

He does not know why the tadpole chose him or what it wants. He has already decided this is a resource problem and begun calculating accordingly.
